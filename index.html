Hello! This is Poulomi* here :)

I am currently a second year PhD candidate in Astronomy at the University of Padova, Italy at the Department of Physics and Astronomy. My research delves into the broad area of galaxy evolution, with a particular focus on understanding the properties of high redshift galaxies (which are definitely not close to us). I am also a part of the Galaxy Evolution and AGN group at Padova.


The goal of my PhD project is to understand the interplay between stars, dust, and the interstellar medium in normal main-sequence galaxies between redshift 4 and 6 which are a sub-sample of the ALPINE galaxies and it is a part of the ALMA large program. To achieve this, I am using observational data by combining deep ALMA [CII] emission maps from higher-resolution ALMA data with JWST data both in spectroscopic and photometric context. I am particularly interested in the resolved studies of these galaxies and understand their formation histories.

I am working under the supervision of Prof. Paolo Cassata at University of Padova and am co-supervised by Prof. Andreas Faisst at California Institute of Technology.

Prior to my doctoral studies, I completed the integrated BS-MS at the Indian Institute of Science Education and Research, Berhmapur with major in Physics and minoring in Biology. The topic of my Master's thesis was "Star Formation Characteristics in Galaxies Hosting Active Galactic Nuclei" where I studied nearby Seyfert galaxies to understand if there is any connection between AGN and the host galaxy which might affect the star formation scenarios at different spatial scales. Moreover, I completed the thesis as a part of the Visiting Research Programme at the Institute of Astrophysics, Bangalore under the supervision of Prof. C. S. Stalin.

Apart from my research, I also try to be involved in the outreach and mentorship activities for people interested in Astronomy. Please refer to the Outreach tab for more info.

Research Projects

As an observational astronomer, my research focuses on understanding the formation and evolution of galaxies across different epochs. Currently, as a PhD student, I am studying the higher redshift galaxies while I have also had the opportunity to work on nearby galaxies previously as an undergraduate.

Spatially Resolved Study of ALPINE Galaxies

Galaxies between redshift 4 and 6 play a crucial role in our understanding of cosmic evolution, particularly in the context of star formation. This epoch marks a period of rapid galaxy growth, where the Star-Formation Rate Density of the Universe peaked, increasing nearly 20 times compared to earlier epochs. Studying these galaxies provides insight into the complex interplay between stars, dust, and the interstellar medium (ISM), which governs galaxy evolution.

In this context, the ALPINE sample which are spectroscopically confirmed main-sequence galaxies in this redshift range, are the ideal dataset for such studies. Having ALMA observations and including [CII] emission with high-resolution imaging from projects like CRISTAL, it is possible to obtain key information on the ISM properties. The recent addition of JWST NIRCam imaging from the COSMOS-Web survey has, for the first time, resolved the optical rest-frame morphology of many of these galaxies. By leveraging multi-wavelength data from HST, JWST, and ALMA, I am trying to build a proper understanding of the various galaxy properties and how these evolved over the cosmic history.

Star Formation Scenarios in Nearby AGN Host Galaxies

The primary goal of the study was to characterize the star forming properties of the hosts of AGN by identifying the star-forming clumps using images from UVIT on-board AstroSat. In this project, I have estimated the star formation rates (SFRs), colours, ages and luminosities along with their variation from the center of the AGN to find any possible trend with distance in eight different sources which are mostly nearby galaxies having redshift less than 0.02. We found that the ratio of SFR in circum-nuclear region to the outer regions were poitively correlated with thE Eddington ratio, which is possibly due to the influence of AGNs.

Relevant Publication:

Payel Nandi, C. S. Stalin, Poulomi Dam and D. J. Saikia ApJ 973 7 (2024)

Nandi et al. 2024

Correlation Analysis between Radio and Optical Variations in OJ-287

OJ287 is a luminous blazar and a key target for studying black holes, as it harbors a supermassive black hole binary at its core. It serves as an excellent case for exploring the connection between radio and optical emissions in active galactic nuclei (AGNs). Under the guidance of Prof. A. Gopakumar at the Tata Institute of Fundamental Research, Mumbai, I worked on a project analyzing the correlation between optical and radio flux variations in OJ287. Our study revealed that optical emissions preceded radio emissions, and we established a relationship linking the observed time delay of maximum correlation with the corresponding radio frequencies: 4.8, 8.0, and 14.5 GHz over a century of optical V-band data.

Outreach

As a person hailing from a small town in India, I strongly feel that science communication and outreach are integral in spreading awareness about one's research and the scientific community at large. Since my undergraduate days, I have tried to engage in several outreach activities whether through public talks, writing, or interactive sessions. I truly enjoy making complex astronomical concepts accessible and engaging. This photograph is from an event organised for high school students and was probably the first time I gave a public outreach talk!


I believe science should be for everyone, and hence, it is important that the outreach activities should be inclusive as well. To support this, I along with my colleagues, have designed a fun activity for the Astronomy Festival (in Italian) titled "Telescopes: Messengers from Space" so that specially abled people could also participate and enjoy learning about science. A full description of the activity can be accessed here.


As a woman in STEM, I also like to take part in initiatives which help to close the gender gap in research. I am a member of CARINAS which is a global non-profit forum of early-career Cosmologists and Astronomers of Indian origin. Specifically, I am a member of the GalPal team which provides mentoring opportunities to masters and bachelors students and also contributing to the upcoming newsletter as a member of the editorial board. Currently, I'm also collaborating on a series called #FirstGenStories, an initiative by Bibiana Prinoth (@astro.bibi_) on instagram which focuses on the amazing and unique stories of first generation PhD students.
